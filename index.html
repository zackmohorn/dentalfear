Reasons for dental fear in children

Dental fear in children is a complex topic and the reasons behind one child’s fear are often very different from the next child. This is because each child perceives, processes, and reacts to stressors differently. Understanding dental fear in children and how to manage it is an important topic because it is often a major reason for behavioral issues in the dental office and a barrier to oral healthcare.

Research studies indicate that many children fear the dentist because of fear of the noise in a dental setting. Specifically, many children fear the sound of a dental drill. Coupled with the bright lights and unfamiliar setting, it is easy to see why noise may add to a child’s overstimulation and anxiety in the dental office. Audiovisual distraction techniques such as watching TV or listening to music through headphones have been found to help relieve much of the anxiety associated with noise at the dentist. Research also shows that having to wait in the reception area, previous dental experience, administration of local anesthesia, and tooth extraction are also major reasons for dental fear in children. Though these procedures are often unavoidable, there are many contributing factors that have been found to influence the degree of fear that the child feels. For example, children often favor a decorated dental clinic as opposed to a plain clinic and prefer that their dentist wear a coat as it helps them associate the dentist with a figure of healing. Studies have indicated that most children prefer a colored coat as opposed to a white one. A positive first dental visit has also been found to lead to less dental fear in subsequent appointments. Though the situation and level of fear will vary with each child, by being aware of the common causes of dental fear in children, parents can be better prepared to help their child deal with their particular fear.

  • The degree and reason for dental fear depends on a particular individual
  • Common reasons for dental fear in children include the sound of the dental drill, waiting in the reception area, previous dental experience, local anesthesia, and tooth extraction
  • Children prefer decorated dentist offices and dentists who dress professionally in a coat
  • Parents who are aware of common reasons for dental fear may be better prepared to deal with their child’s dental anxiety
